# ASRS v1.1: Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale

## What the ASRS Screens For

The Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale version 1.1 (ASRS v1.1) is a screening tool developed by the World Health Organization (WHO) in collaboration with leading ADHD researchers. It screens for attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) in adults aged 18 and older. The ASRS is not a diagnostic instrument. It identifies individuals whose symptoms are consistent with ADHD and who would benefit from a comprehensive diagnostic evaluation.

ADHD in adults is characterized by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that interfere with functioning. Unlike the childhood presentation that is often marked by visible hyperactivity, adult ADHD frequently manifests as internal restlessness, chronic difficulty with organization and follow-through, emotional dysregulation, and problems sustaining attention on tasks that are not inherently stimulating.

## How the ASRS Is Structured

The full ASRS contains 18 items corresponding to the DSM-5 criteria for ADHD. The first six items form Part A, which serves as the primary screener. Part B (items 7-18) provides additional information about symptom frequency but is not used in the initial screening determination.

Each item asks how often a particular behavior or experience occurs, rated on a five-point scale: never (0), rarely (1), sometimes (2), often (3), very often (4).

## How the Per-Item Threshold Scoring Works

The ASRS uses a unique scoring approach that differs from most screening tools. Rather than simply summing all item scores, each item has its own threshold that determines whether it counts as a positive screen indicator. This is because different ADHD symptoms have different base rates in the general population.

For Part A, some items have a threshold of "sometimes" or higher (a score of 2 or more counts as positive), while other items have a higher threshold of "often" or higher (a score of 3 or more counts as positive). The specific thresholds were set based on research examining which cutpoints best discriminated between adults with and without ADHD for each individual symptom.

The items with lower thresholds (sometimes or higher) tend to be behaviors that are less common in the general population, so even moderate frequency is noteworthy. Items with higher thresholds (often or higher) assess behaviors that many people experience occasionally, so a higher frequency is needed for the item to be clinically meaningful.

A positive screen on Part A is indicated when four or more of the six items meet or exceed their individual thresholds. This scoring method was found to have better predictive accuracy than a simple sum score approach.

## What a Positive Screen Means

A positive ASRS screen means that the pattern and frequency of reported symptoms are consistent with adult ADHD and that further evaluation is warranted. It does not mean ADHD is present.

Several important considerations apply:

### It Is a Starting Point, Not an Endpoint

The ASRS identifies people who should undergo a comprehensive ADHD evaluation. A full evaluation typically includes a detailed developmental and symptom history, assessment of functional impairment across settings, consideration of when symptoms began (ADHD symptoms must have been present before age 12), collateral information from family members or partners when available, and ruling out other conditions that can mimic ADHD symptoms.

### Other Conditions Can Produce Similar Symptoms

Anxiety disorders, depression, sleep disorders, thyroid dysfunction, substance use, and certain medications can cause inattention, restlessness, and concentration difficulties that overlap with ADHD symptoms. A thorough evaluation differentiates ADHD from these conditions or identifies when they co-occur.

### ADHD Often Co-occurs with Other Conditions

In adults with ADHD, comorbidity is the rule rather than the exception. Common co-occurring conditions include anxiety disorders, depression, substance use disorders, and learning disabilities. A comprehensive evaluation assesses for these as well.

## What a Negative Screen Means

A negative screen (fewer than four items meeting threshold on Part A) suggests that ADHD is less likely but does not definitively rule it out. Some individuals with ADHD, particularly those who have developed strong compensatory strategies or whose symptoms are primarily inattentive rather than hyperactive, may not screen positive. If clinical suspicion remains despite a negative screen, a full evaluation is still appropriate.

## Next Steps After a Positive Screen

### Seek a Comprehensive Evaluation

The recommended next step is an evaluation by a clinician experienced in diagnosing adult ADHD. This may be a psychiatrist, psychologist, neuropsychologist, or other qualified mental health professional. The evaluation should include a thorough clinical interview, not just questionnaires.

### Gather Supporting Information

Before the evaluation, it can be helpful to reflect on childhood functioning (report cards, parental observations), document current areas of difficulty and their impact on work, relationships, and daily life, and note any strategies that have or have not helped manage symptoms.

### Understand Treatment Options

If ADHD is diagnosed, evidence-based treatments include stimulant and non-stimulant medications, cognitive behavioral therapy adapted for ADHD, skills training focused on organization, time management, and emotional regulation, and environmental modifications such as structured routines and external reminders. Many adults benefit from a combination of medication and behavioral strategies.

### Consider the Full Picture

A positive screen is an opportunity to understand patterns that may have been causing difficulty for years. Many adults with ADHD report feeling relief at having an explanation for longstanding struggles with organization, follow-through, and consistency. Understanding the condition is itself a meaningful step toward better functioning.
