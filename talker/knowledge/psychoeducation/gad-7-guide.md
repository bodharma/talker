# GAD-7: Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-Item Scale

## What the GAD-7 Measures

The GAD-7 is a seven-item screening tool that measures the severity of generalized anxiety symptoms over the past two weeks. Originally developed to screen for generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), it has also demonstrated good sensitivity for detecting panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, and post-traumatic stress disorder, making it a useful general anxiety screener.

The seven items assess: feeling nervous or on edge, inability to stop or control worrying, worrying too much about different things, trouble relaxing, restlessness, irritability, and feeling afraid as if something awful might happen.

Each item is rated from 0 to 3 based on frequency: 0 (not at all), 1 (several days), 2 (more than half the days), 3 (nearly every day). Total scores range from 0 to 21.

## Score Ranges and What They Mean

### Minimal Anxiety (0-4)

Scores in this range indicate few or no anxiety symptoms. Everyday worries may be present, but they are manageable and do not interfere with functioning. No clinical intervention is needed, though awareness of anxiety triggers and basic stress management practices remain beneficial.

### Mild Anxiety (5-9)

This range suggests noticeable anxiety that may cause some discomfort but is not yet significantly disruptive. A person might feel more on edge than usual, have difficulty relaxing after work, or notice increased worry about everyday situations. Self-management strategies such as regular physical activity, mindfulness practices, adequate sleep, and reduced caffeine intake can be helpful. Monitoring over time is recommended to ensure symptoms do not escalate.

### Moderate Anxiety (10-14)

Moderate scores indicate anxiety symptoms that are likely interfering with daily functioning. Work performance may suffer due to difficulty concentrating. Social situations may feel more draining or be avoided. Physical symptoms such as muscle tension, headaches, or gastrointestinal discomfort may be present. Clinical evaluation is recommended. Evidence-based treatments, including cognitive behavioral therapy and in some cases medication, are effective at this severity level.

### Severe Anxiety (15-21)

Scores in this range reflect intense and pervasive anxiety that significantly impairs daily life. A person may find it very difficult to concentrate, may avoid situations that trigger worry, and may experience significant physical symptoms. Sleep is often disrupted, and the worry can feel uncontrollable. Professional treatment is strongly recommended, typically involving a combination of psychotherapy and medication. Referral to a mental health specialist is appropriate.

## How Anxiety Affects Functioning

Anxiety does not just live in the mind. It is a whole-body experience with impacts across multiple domains of functioning.

### Cognitive Effects

Persistent worry consumes mental bandwidth. Concentration suffers, decision-making becomes difficult, and the mind gravitates toward worst-case scenarios. People often describe a feeling of mental fog or an inability to "turn off" their thoughts. Memory and problem-solving can also be affected.

### Physical Effects

The body's stress response system stays activated. Common physical manifestations include muscle tension (especially in the neck, shoulders, and jaw), headaches, stomach problems, rapid heartbeat, shortness of breath, fatigue, and sleep disturbance. Many people with anxiety initially present to their doctor with physical complaints rather than emotional ones.

### Social and Occupational Effects

Anxiety can lead to avoidance of situations that trigger worry. This might mean declining social invitations, avoiding speaking up at work, procrastinating on tasks that feel overwhelming, or not pursuing opportunities that involve uncertainty. Over time, this avoidance can narrow a person's life and reinforce the anxiety cycle.

### Relationship Effects

Irritability and restlessness can strain relationships. Excessive reassurance-seeking, difficulty being present in conversations, and avoidance of conflict can create distance between partners, friends, or family members. Some people withdraw socially, while others become overly dependent on others for comfort.

## The Difference Between Normal Worry and Clinical Anxiety

Everyone worries. Normal worry tends to be proportional to the situation, time-limited, and does not significantly impair functioning. Clinical anxiety is distinguished by its intensity, duration, and impact. The worry feels difficult or impossible to control, persists across situations, and interferes with the ability to function effectively in daily life. The GAD-7 helps quantify this distinction.

## What an Elevated Score Means

An elevated GAD-7 score is not a diagnosis. It is an indication that anxiety symptoms are present at a level that warrants attention. Several medical conditions can mimic or worsen anxiety, including thyroid disorders, cardiac arrhythmias, and certain medications. A thorough evaluation includes considering these possibilities.

For people already in treatment, the GAD-7 tracks symptom change over time. A decrease of 4 or more points is generally considered a clinically meaningful improvement.

## When to Seek Help

Professional evaluation is recommended when:

- The total score is 10 or above
- Anxiety is interfering with work, school, or relationships
- Physical symptoms of anxiety are persistent or distressing
- Avoidance behaviors are limiting daily activities
- Anxiety is accompanied by depressive symptoms
- Substance use has increased as a way to manage anxiety
- The worry feels uncontrollable despite efforts to manage it
