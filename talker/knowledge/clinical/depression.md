# Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

## DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria

Major Depressive Disorder requires five or more of the following symptoms present during the same two-week period, representing a change from previous functioning. At least one symptom must be either depressed mood or loss of interest/pleasure (anhedonia).

1. **Depressed mood** most of the day, nearly every day, as indicated by subjective report (feels sad, empty, hopeless) or observation by others. In children and adolescents, can be irritable mood.
2. **Markedly diminished interest or pleasure** (anhedonia) in all, or almost all, activities most of the day, nearly every day.
3. **Significant weight change** — weight loss when not dieting or weight gain (more than 5% of body weight in a month), or decrease or increase in appetite nearly every day.
4. **Insomnia or hypersomnia** nearly every day.
5. **Psychomotor agitation or retardation** nearly every day, observable by others (not merely subjective feelings of restlessness or being slowed down).
6. **Fatigue or loss of energy** nearly every day.
7. **Feelings of worthlessness or excessive/inappropriate guilt** (which may be delusional), nearly every day — not merely self-reproach or guilt about being sick.
8. **Diminished ability to think or concentrate**, or indecisiveness, nearly every day.
9. **Recurrent thoughts of death** (not just fear of dying), recurrent suicidal ideation without a specific plan, a suicide attempt, or a specific plan for committing suicide.

The symptoms must cause clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning. The episode is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition. The episode is not better explained by schizoaffective disorder, schizophrenia, or other psychotic disorders. There has never been a manic or hypomanic episode (which would indicate a bipolar spectrum disorder).

### Severity Specifiers

- **Mild**: Few symptoms beyond the minimum required, minor functional impairment.
- **Moderate**: Symptom count and functional impairment between mild and severe.
- **Severe**: Substantially more symptoms than required, serious functional impairment. May include psychotic features (mood-congruent or mood-incongruent delusions or hallucinations).

## Subtypes and Specifiers

### Melancholic Features

Characterized by a profound loss of pleasure in virtually all activities, a distinctly depressed mood qualitatively different from grief, depression regularly worse in the morning, early morning awakening (at least two hours before usual), marked psychomotor agitation or retardation, significant anorexia or weight loss, and excessive or inappropriate guilt. Melancholic depression tends to respond well to biological treatments (medication, ECT) and less well to psychotherapy alone.

### Atypical Features

Defined by mood reactivity (mood brightens in response to positive events) plus two or more of: significant weight gain or increase in appetite, hypersomnia, leaden paralysis (heavy, leaden feelings in arms or legs lasting an hour or more daily), and a long-standing pattern of interpersonal rejection sensitivity that causes significant social or occupational impairment. Atypical depression often has an earlier age of onset and a more chronic course. MAOIs and SSRIs tend to be more effective than tricyclic antidepressants for this subtype.

### Seasonal Pattern (Seasonal Affective Disorder)

A regular temporal relationship between onset of major depressive episodes and a particular time of year (most commonly fall/winter). Full remissions or a change from depression to mania/hypomania also occur at a characteristic time of year (typically spring). At least two major depressive episodes in the last two years must demonstrate this temporal relationship, and seasonal episodes must substantially outnumber nonseasonal episodes over the lifetime. Light therapy is an established first-line treatment.

### Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)

Depressed mood for most of the day, for more days than not, for at least two years (one year in children/adolescents). During this period, two or more of the following are present: poor appetite or overeating, insomnia or hypersomnia, low energy or fatigue, low self-esteem, poor concentration or difficulty making decisions, feelings of hopelessness. The individual has not been without symptoms for more than two months at a time during the two-year period. When a major depressive episode is superimposed on persistent depressive disorder, this is sometimes called "double depression."

### Peripartum Onset

Major depressive episode with onset during pregnancy or within four weeks following delivery (though clinically, the peripartum window is often extended to the first year postpartum). Affects approximately 10-15% of women. Must be distinguished from postpartum blues (transient mood lability in the first two weeks, affecting up to 80% of new mothers).

## Differential Diagnosis

### Bipolar Disorder
A critical differential. Patients with bipolar II disorder spend the majority of their illness in depressive episodes and may initially present as having MDD. A thorough history of any hypomanic or manic episodes, family history of bipolar disorder, early onset (teens or early twenties), rapid cycling, and antidepressant-induced mania should be assessed. Treating bipolar depression with antidepressants alone can precipitate mania.

### Medical Conditions
Hypothyroidism, anemia, vitamin B12 or folate deficiency, sleep apnea, chronic fatigue syndrome, neurological conditions (Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, stroke), and chronic pain syndromes can all mimic or contribute to depressive symptoms. Basic laboratory workup (TSH, CBC, metabolic panel, B12) is standard practice when evaluating new-onset depression.

### Substance-Induced Depressive Disorder
Alcohol, sedatives, opioids, and stimulant withdrawal can produce depressive syndromes. Corticosteroids, interferon-alpha, and some antihypertensives are also associated with depression. Onset during or shortly after substance use, intoxication, or withdrawal suggests this diagnosis.

### Adjustment Disorder with Depressed Mood
Depressive symptoms developing within three months of an identifiable stressor that do not meet the full criteria for MDD. Symptoms resolve within six months after the stressor or its consequences have ended.

### Grief and Bereavement
Normal grief can include sadness, insomnia, poor appetite, and weight loss. Key distinguishing features of MDD versus grief include: pervasive feelings of worthlessness, suicidal ideation beyond wanting to join the deceased, psychomotor retardation, prolonged and marked functional impairment, and hallucinatory experiences other than hearing or seeing the deceased.

## Prevalence and Risk Factors

### Prevalence
The 12-month prevalence of MDD in the United States is approximately 7%, with the highest rates in the 18-29 age group. Lifetime prevalence is approximately 16-20%. The female-to-male ratio is approximately 2:1, a difference that emerges during adolescence.

### Risk Factors
- **Genetic**: First-degree relatives of individuals with MDD have a two- to four-fold increased risk. Heritability is estimated at 37-40%.
- **Temperamental**: Neuroticism (negative affectivity) is a well-established risk factor.
- **Environmental**: Adverse childhood experiences (abuse, neglect, parental loss), chronic stress, social isolation, and low socioeconomic status.
- **Comorbid conditions**: Anxiety disorders, substance use disorders, chronic medical illness, and chronic pain substantially increase risk.
- **Prior episodes**: The strongest predictor of a future depressive episode is a history of previous episodes. After a first episode, the recurrence rate is approximately 50%; after three or more episodes, it exceeds 90%.

## Course and Prognosis

The average duration of an untreated major depressive episode is six to thirteen months; most treated episodes remit within three months. Approximately 20% of episodes become chronic (lasting two or more years). Roughly two-thirds of patients achieve full remission with adequate treatment; the remaining third achieve partial remission or remain treatment-resistant. Early onset, comorbid anxiety, comorbid personality disorder, and greater initial severity predict a more chronic course.
