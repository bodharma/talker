# Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) in Adults

## DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria

ADHD is defined by a persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development. For adults (age 17 and older), five or more symptoms from either the inattention or hyperactivity-impulsivity domain must be present for at least six months to a degree that is inconsistent with developmental level and directly negatively impacts social, academic, or occupational activities.

### Inattention Symptoms

1. **Fails to give close attention to details** or makes careless mistakes in work or other activities.
2. **Difficulty sustaining attention** in tasks or play activities (e.g., difficulty remaining focused during lectures, conversations, or lengthy reading).
3. **Does not seem to listen** when spoken to directly (mind seems elsewhere, even in the absence of obvious distraction).
4. **Does not follow through on instructions** and fails to finish work duties or obligations (starts tasks but quickly loses focus and is easily sidetracked).
5. **Difficulty organizing tasks and activities** (difficulty managing sequential tasks, keeping materials and belongings in order, messy or disorganized work, poor time management, fails to meet deadlines).
6. **Avoids, dislikes, or is reluctant to engage** in tasks that require sustained mental effort (e.g., preparing reports, completing forms, reviewing lengthy papers).
7. **Often loses things** necessary for tasks and activities (e.g., keys, wallet, phone, paperwork, eyeglasses).
8. **Easily distracted** by extraneous stimuli (for older adolescents and adults, may include unrelated thoughts).
9. **Forgetful in daily activities** (e.g., paying bills, keeping appointments, returning calls).

### Hyperactivity-Impulsivity Symptoms

1. **Fidgets with or taps hands or feet** or squirms in seat.
2. **Leaves seat** in situations when remaining seated is expected.
3. **Runs about or climbs** in inappropriate situations (in adults, may be limited to feeling restless).
4. **Unable to play or engage in leisure activities quietly**.
5. **Often "on the go"** or acts as if "driven by a motor" (uncomfortable being still for extended time, as in restaurants or meetings; experienced by others as being restless or difficult to keep up with).
6. **Talks excessively**.
7. **Blurts out answers** before a question has been completed (finishes others' sentences, cannot wait for turn in conversation).
8. **Difficulty waiting turn** (e.g., while waiting in line).
9. **Interrupts or intrudes on others** (butts into conversations, games, or activities; may start using other people's things without asking or receiving permission).

### Additional Criteria

- Several inattentive or hyperactive-impulsive symptoms were present **before age 12**.
- Symptoms are present in **two or more settings** (home, work, with friends or relatives, other activities).
- Symptoms **interfere with or reduce the quality** of social, academic, or occupational functioning.
- Symptoms are **not better explained** by another mental disorder (e.g., mood disorder, anxiety disorder, dissociative disorder, personality disorder, substance intoxication or withdrawal).

## Presentation Types

### Predominantly Inattentive Presentation

Five or more inattention symptoms but fewer than five hyperactivity-impulsivity symptoms. This is the most commonly diagnosed presentation in adults, particularly adult women. Individuals with this presentation are often described as "daydreamers" — they may appear sluggish, slow to process information, and are more likely to be overlooked or misdiagnosed because they do not exhibit the disruptive behavior associated with hyperactivity.

### Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive Presentation

Five or more hyperactivity-impulsivity symptoms but fewer than five inattention symptoms. This presentation is relatively uncommon in adults and is more typically seen in young children. Most individuals with this childhood presentation develop sufficient inattention symptoms over time to meet criteria for the combined presentation.

### Combined Presentation

Five or more symptoms from both the inattention and hyperactivity-impulsivity domains. This is the most common presentation overall and frequently persists into adulthood.

## How Adult ADHD Differs from Childhood ADHD

### Symptom Evolution

Hyperactivity tends to diminish with age, often transforming from overt physical overactivity in children to an internal sense of restlessness, mental hyperactivity, or difficulty relaxing in adults. Adults may describe feeling "driven" or unable to unwind rather than exhibiting the running, climbing, and constant motion seen in children.

Impulsivity may shift from physical impulsivity (grabbing things, running into traffic) to verbal impulsivity (interrupting, blurting out), impulsive decision-making (quitting jobs, making impulsive purchases, starting and abandoning projects), and emotional impulsivity (rapid mood shifts, low frustration tolerance, impatience).

Inattention symptoms tend to remain the most stable across the lifespan and often become more impairing as adult responsibilities increase. Tasks that were previously structured by parents and teachers (organizing schedules, meeting deadlines, managing finances) now fall to the individual.

### Functional Impairment in Adults

Adult ADHD impairment manifests differently than in children:

- **Occupational**: Difficulty meeting deadlines, disorganized work, frequent job changes, underperformance relative to ability, difficulty in meetings requiring sustained attention.
- **Academic**: Difficulty completing degrees, poor study habits, academic achievement below measured intellectual ability.
- **Financial**: Impulsive spending, difficulty with bills and budgeting, poor long-term financial planning.
- **Interpersonal**: Forgetting commitments, appearing not to listen to partners, difficulty maintaining relationships, emotional reactivity in conflicts.
- **Driving**: Higher rates of traffic violations, accidents, and license suspensions.
- **Health management**: Difficulty adhering to medical regimens, missed appointments, inconsistent self-care routines.

### Diagnostic Challenges in Adults

Adult diagnosis requires retrospective confirmation of childhood symptom onset (before age 12), which can be difficult to establish. Collateral information from parents, school records, or childhood report cards is valuable. Many adults, particularly those with the inattentive presentation and higher intellectual ability, develop compensatory strategies that mask symptoms until demands exceed their capacity (e.g., transition to college, career advancement, parenthood).

### Emotional Dysregulation

Although not part of the DSM-5 diagnostic criteria, emotional dysregulation is now widely recognized as a core feature of ADHD. This includes low frustration tolerance, irritability, emotional overreactivity, difficulty calming down after emotional arousal, and rapid mood shifts (typically lasting minutes to hours, not days). Emotional dysregulation in ADHD is distinct from the mood episodes of bipolar disorder and the interpersonal instability of borderline personality disorder, though differential diagnosis can be challenging.

## Common Co-occurring Conditions

### Mood Disorders
Major depressive disorder co-occurs with adult ADHD in approximately 20-30% of cases. It can be difficult to distinguish primary depressive symptoms from the demoralization that results from chronic ADHD-related underachievement and interpersonal difficulties. Bipolar disorder co-occurs at elevated rates (approximately 10-20% of ADHD adults), and differentiating ADHD from bipolar II hypomania requires careful longitudinal history.

### Anxiety Disorders
Approximately 25-50% of adults with ADHD have a comorbid anxiety disorder. GAD is particularly common. Anxiety in ADHD may be secondary to the chronic stress of managing ADHD symptoms (missed deadlines, interpersonal friction) or may represent an independent comorbid condition.

### Substance Use Disorders
Adults with ADHD have approximately twice the rate of substance use disorders compared to the general population. Stimulant use, alcohol use, and cannabis use are particularly elevated. ADHD treatment with stimulant medication in childhood appears to be protective against later substance use disorders rather than increasing risk.

### Sleep Disorders
Up to 70-80% of adults with ADHD report sleep difficulties, including delayed sleep phase, difficulty falling asleep due to racing thoughts, restless sleep, and difficulty waking. Sleep deprivation exacerbates ADHD symptoms, creating a cyclical pattern.

### Learning Disabilities
Specific learning disorders (reading, writing, mathematics) co-occur with ADHD at elevated rates and contribute to academic underachievement beyond what ADHD alone would predict.

## Prevalence

The prevalence of ADHD in children is approximately 5-7% worldwide. In adults, prevalence is estimated at 2.5-4.4%, reflecting both genuine remission in some individuals and persistent symptoms with diminished hyperactivity in others. Approximately 50-65% of children with ADHD continue to meet full or partial criteria in adulthood.

Historically, ADHD was diagnosed more frequently in males (childhood male-to-female ratio of approximately 3:1 in community samples). In adults, the sex ratio narrows considerably to approximately 1.5:1, likely reflecting increased recognition that the predominantly inattentive presentation — more common in females — was historically underdiagnosed. Many women receive their first ADHD diagnosis in their 30s or 40s.
