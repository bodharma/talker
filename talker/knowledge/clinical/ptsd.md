# Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

## DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria

PTSD in the DSM-5 is classified under Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders, not Anxiety Disorders (a change from DSM-IV). The diagnosis requires exposure to a qualifying traumatic event and symptoms across four distinct clusters.

### Criterion A — Trauma Exposure

Exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence in one or more of the following ways:

1. **Directly experiencing** the traumatic event(s)
2. **Witnessing**, in person, the event(s) as it occurred to others
3. **Learning that** the traumatic event(s) occurred to a close family member or close friend (in cases of actual or threatened death, the event must have been violent or accidental)
4. **Experiencing repeated or extreme exposure** to aversive details of the traumatic event(s) — for example, first responders collecting human remains, police officers repeatedly exposed to details of child abuse (does not apply to exposure through electronic media, television, movies, or pictures, unless work-related)

### Criterion B — Intrusion Symptoms (1 or more required)

The traumatic event is persistently re-experienced in one or more of the following ways:

1. **Recurrent, involuntary, and intrusive distressing memories** of the traumatic event(s). In children older than six, repetitive play may occur in which themes or aspects of the traumatic event are expressed.
2. **Recurrent distressing dreams** in which the content and/or affect of the dream are related to the traumatic event(s). In children, there may be frightening dreams without recognizable content.
3. **Dissociative reactions (flashbacks)** in which the individual feels or acts as if the traumatic event were recurring. These occur on a continuum from brief episodes to complete loss of awareness of present surroundings.
4. **Intense or prolonged psychological distress** at exposure to internal or external cues that symbolize or resemble an aspect of the traumatic event(s).
5. **Marked physiological reactions** to internal or external cues that symbolize or resemble an aspect of the traumatic event(s).

### Criterion C — Avoidance (1 or more required)

Persistent avoidance of stimuli associated with the traumatic event(s), beginning after the traumatic event occurred:

1. **Avoidance of or efforts to avoid distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings** about or closely associated with the traumatic event(s).
2. **Avoidance of or efforts to avoid external reminders** (people, places, conversations, activities, objects, situations) that arouse distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about or closely associated with the traumatic event(s).

### Criterion D — Negative Alterations in Cognitions and Mood (2 or more required)

Negative alterations in cognitions and mood associated with the traumatic event(s), beginning or worsening after the traumatic event occurred:

1. **Inability to remember** an important aspect of the traumatic event(s) — typically due to dissociative amnesia, not head injury, alcohol, or drugs.
2. **Persistent and exaggerated negative beliefs or expectations** about oneself, others, or the world (e.g., "I am bad," "No one can be trusted," "The world is completely dangerous," "My whole nervous system is permanently ruined").
3. **Persistent, distorted cognitions** about the cause or consequences of the traumatic event(s) that lead the individual to blame themselves or others.
4. **Persistent negative emotional state** — fear, horror, anger, guilt, or shame.
5. **Markedly diminished interest or participation** in significant activities.
6. **Feelings of detachment or estrangement** from others.
7. **Persistent inability to experience positive emotions** — inability to experience happiness, satisfaction, or loving feelings (emotional numbing).

### Criterion E — Alterations in Arousal and Reactivity (2 or more required)

Marked alterations in arousal and reactivity associated with the traumatic event(s), beginning or worsening after the traumatic event occurred:

1. **Irritable behavior and angry outbursts** (with little or no provocation), typically expressed as verbal or physical aggression toward people or objects.
2. **Reckless or self-destructive behavior**.
3. **Hypervigilance**.
4. **Exaggerated startle response**.
5. **Problems with concentration**.
6. **Sleep disturbance** — difficulty falling or staying asleep, or restless sleep.

### Additional Criteria

- **Duration**: Symptoms in Criteria B, C, D, and E must persist for more than one month (Criterion F).
- **Functional impairment**: The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning (Criterion G).
- **Exclusion**: The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition (Criterion H).

### Specifiers

- **With dissociative symptoms**: The individual's symptoms meet PTSD criteria and, in response to the stressor, the individual also experiences persistent or recurrent depersonalization (feeling detached from one's own mind or body) or derealization (experiences of unreality of surroundings). The dissociative subtype occurs in approximately 14-30% of PTSD cases.
- **With delayed expression**: Full diagnostic criteria are not met until at least six months after the event, although onset and expression of some symptoms may be immediate.

## Types of Qualifying Trauma

Qualifying traumatic events under Criterion A include but are not limited to:

- **Combat exposure and military trauma** — direct combat, improvised explosive device blasts, witnessing death of comrades
- **Sexual assault and rape** — one of the most potent trauma types for PTSD development, with conditional risk estimates of 30-50%
- **Physical assault** — mugging, robbery, being attacked or beaten
- **Childhood abuse** — physical abuse, sexual abuse, severe neglect
- **Domestic violence** — ongoing intimate partner violence
- **Serious accidents** — motor vehicle collisions, industrial accidents, severe burns
- **Natural disasters** — earthquakes, hurricanes, floods, wildfires
- **Medical trauma** — life-threatening medical events, ICU stays, traumatic childbirth
- **Witnessing violence** — seeing someone killed or seriously injured
- **Terrorism and mass violence** — bombings, mass shootings
- **Torture and captivity** — prisoner of war, kidnapping, human trafficking
- **Professional exposure** — first responders, military personnel, forensic professionals repeatedly exposed to trauma material

Events that do not typically meet Criterion A include divorce, job loss, financial hardship, non-life-threatening illness, and interpersonal conflict. While these can cause significant distress and adjustment difficulties, they are generally classified under adjustment disorders rather than PTSD.

## Complex PTSD

### Conceptual Framework

Complex PTSD (C-PTSD) is recognized by the ICD-11 (but not the DSM-5 as a separate diagnosis) and arises from prolonged, repeated trauma from which escape is difficult or impossible. Typical contexts include prolonged childhood abuse, domestic violence, torture, captivity, and human trafficking.

### Core Features Beyond Standard PTSD

In addition to the three core PTSD symptom clusters recognized in ICD-11 (re-experiencing, avoidance, and hyperarousal), Complex PTSD adds three domains of "disturbances in self-organization":

1. **Affect dysregulation**: Difficulty modulating emotional responses, including heightened emotional reactivity, explosive anger, or emotional numbing. May include chronic suicidal ideation and self-harm.
2. **Negative self-concept**: Persistent feelings of being diminished, defeated, or worthless. Pervasive shame and guilt. Fundamentally altered identity and sense of self.
3. **Interpersonal difficulties**: Difficulty feeling close to others, persistent distrust, repeated patterns of revictimization, difficulty maintaining stable relationships.

### Clinical Relevance

Although the DSM-5 does not include a separate C-PTSD diagnosis, many of these features are captured within the Criterion D (cognitions and mood) symptoms and may be further captured by comorbid diagnoses such as borderline personality disorder, dissociative disorders, or major depression. Recognition of complex trauma presentations is important because these individuals often require longer treatment courses, may respond differently to standard PTSD protocols, and frequently have extensive comorbidity.

## Prevalence

### General Population
The lifetime prevalence of PTSD in the United States is approximately 6.1-6.8%. The 12-month prevalence is approximately 3.5%. Globally, lifetime prevalence varies considerably by region, ranging from approximately 1% in some East Asian countries to 7-8% in North America.

### Sex Differences
PTSD is approximately twice as common in women as in men, despite men having higher overall rates of trauma exposure. This difference is partly attributable to the types of trauma women are more likely to experience (sexual assault carries a particularly high conditional risk for PTSD) and partly to biological and psychosocial factors.

### High-Risk Populations
- **Combat veterans**: Lifetime prevalence of 10-30% depending on era, combat exposure level, and assessment method.
- **Sexual assault survivors**: Conditional risk (probability of PTSD given trauma exposure) of 30-50%.
- **First responders**: Prevalence estimates of 7-37% depending on role and exposure.
- **Refugees and displaced persons**: Prevalence estimates of 20-40% in many studied populations.

### Risk and Protective Factors

**Pre-trauma risk factors**: Prior trauma exposure, prior mental health conditions, family history of psychopathology, lower socioeconomic status, younger age at trauma, female sex, lower cognitive ability, and personality traits such as neuroticism.

**Peri-trauma risk factors**: Greater perceived life threat, greater severity of trauma, dissociation during or immediately after the event, and traumatic brain injury.

**Post-trauma risk factors**: Lack of social support (one of the strongest predictors), subsequent life stress, maladaptive coping strategies, and lack of access to treatment.

**Protective factors**: Strong social support, higher education, pre-existing psychological resilience, and early intervention after trauma exposure.
