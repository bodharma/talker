# Anxiety Disorders

## Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) — DSM-5 Criteria

### Diagnostic Criteria

A. **Excessive anxiety and worry** (apprehensive expectation) occurring more days than not for at least six months, about a number of events or activities (such as work or school performance).

B. The individual finds it **difficult to control the worry**.

C. The anxiety and worry are associated with **three or more** of the following six symptoms (with at least some symptoms present for more days than not for the past six months). Only one item is required in children.

1. **Restlessness** or feeling keyed up or on edge
2. **Being easily fatigued**
3. **Difficulty concentrating** or mind going blank
4. **Irritability**
5. **Muscle tension**
6. **Sleep disturbance** (difficulty falling or staying asleep, or restless, unsatisfying sleep)

D. The anxiety, worry, or physical symptoms cause **clinically significant distress** or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

E. The disturbance is **not attributable** to the physiological effects of a substance or another medical condition.

F. The disturbance is **not better explained** by another mental disorder (e.g., anxiety about having panic attacks in panic disorder, negative evaluation in social anxiety disorder, contamination in OCD, separation from attachment figures in separation anxiety disorder, reminders of traumatic events in PTSD, gaining weight in anorexia nervosa, physical complaints in somatic symptom disorder, perceived appearance flaws in body dysmorphic disorder, having a serious illness in illness anxiety disorder, or the content of delusional beliefs in schizophrenia or delusional disorder).

### Key Features

The hallmark of GAD is chronic, diffuse worry that shifts from topic to topic. Patients with GAD often describe themselves as lifelong worriers. The worry is experienced as difficult to control and is disproportionate to the actual likelihood or impact of anticipated events. GAD differs from normal worry in its pervasiveness, intensity, duration, and the degree of associated distress and impairment.

## Panic Disorder — DSM-5 Criteria

### Diagnostic Criteria

A. **Recurrent unexpected panic attacks** — an abrupt surge of intense fear or intense discomfort that reaches a peak within minutes, during which four or more of the following symptoms occur:

1. Palpitations, pounding heart, or accelerated heart rate
2. Sweating
3. Trembling or shaking
4. Sensations of shortness of breath or smothering
5. Feelings of choking
6. Chest pain or discomfort
7. Nausea or abdominal distress
8. Feeling dizzy, unsteady, light-headed, or faint
9. Chills or heat sensations
10. Paresthesias (numbness or tingling sensations)
11. Derealization (feelings of unreality) or depersonalization (being detached from oneself)
12. Fear of losing control or "going crazy"
13. Fear of dying

B. At least one attack has been followed by **one month or more** of one or both of the following:
- Persistent concern or worry about additional panic attacks or their consequences (e.g., losing control, having a heart attack, "going crazy")
- A significant maladaptive change in behavior related to the attacks (e.g., avoidance of exercise, unfamiliar situations, or leaving the home)

## Panic Disorder vs. Generalized Anxiety Disorder

### Key Distinctions

| Feature | Panic Disorder | GAD |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| **Onset of anxiety** | Sudden, acute surges (panic attacks) | Gradual, chronic, persistent |
| **Duration of episodes** | Minutes (peak within 10 minutes) | Hours to days, ongoing for months |
| **Physical symptoms** | Intense, clustered (cardiac, respiratory) | Diffuse (muscle tension, fatigue, restlessness) |
| **Focus of fear** | Immediate catastrophe (heart attack, dying, losing control) | Future events, everyday concerns |
| **Between episodes** | Anticipatory anxiety about next attack | Continuous baseline worry |
| **Avoidance** | Situation-specific (places where attacks occurred) | Less patterned; may avoid decisions or situations that provoke worry |

### Overlap

Panic disorder and GAD frequently co-occur. Approximately 25% of individuals with panic disorder also meet criteria for GAD. When both are present, both diagnoses should be assigned. In clinical assessment, it is important to distinguish the chronic worry of GAD from the anticipatory anxiety of panic disorder (which is specifically focused on having future panic attacks).

## Somatic Symptoms of Anxiety

Anxiety disorders frequently present with prominent somatic complaints, which may be the primary reason a person seeks help. Understanding these presentations is critical for accurate assessment.

### Cardiovascular
Palpitations, tachycardia, chest tightness or pain, sensation of skipped heartbeats. These symptoms frequently lead to emergency department visits and cardiology referrals.

### Respiratory
Shortness of breath, hyperventilation, air hunger, sighing respirations. Hyperventilation can produce secondary symptoms including dizziness, tingling in extremities, and lightheadedness through respiratory alkalosis.

### Gastrointestinal
Nausea, diarrhea, abdominal cramping, dry mouth, difficulty swallowing (globus sensation), irritable bowel symptoms. There is a strong bidirectional relationship between anxiety and functional GI disorders.

### Musculoskeletal
Chronic muscle tension (especially neck, shoulders, jaw), tension headaches, temporomandibular joint pain, tremor.

### Neurological
Dizziness, lightheadedness, paresthesias, blurred vision, tinnitus, difficulty concentrating, depersonalization, derealization.

### Autonomic
Sweating, flushing, chills, frequent urination, sexual dysfunction.

## Differential Diagnosis from Medical Conditions

### Thyroid Disease
Hyperthyroidism produces anxiety, tremor, palpitations, weight loss, heat intolerance, and irritability that closely mimics GAD or panic disorder. TSH screening is essential when evaluating new-onset anxiety, particularly with unexplained weight loss or heat intolerance.

### Cardiac Arrhythmias
Supraventricular tachycardia, atrial fibrillation, and mitral valve prolapse can produce episodic palpitations, chest discomfort, and breathlessness that mimic panic attacks. An ECG, and in some cases Holter monitoring, may be indicated.

### Pheochromocytoma
Rare but important to consider — produces episodic hypertension, headache, sweating, and palpitations that can closely mimic panic attacks. Suspect in cases with associated hypertension or when episodes occur with exertion or positional changes.

### Pulmonary Conditions
Asthma, COPD, and pulmonary embolism can produce breathlessness and chest tightness that overlap with panic symptoms. Recurrent episodes of air hunger warrant pulmonary evaluation.

### Substance-Related Causes
Caffeine excess, stimulant use (amphetamines, cocaine), cannabis (particularly high-THC strains), alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal, and sympathomimetic medications (decongestants, bronchodilators, thyroid supplements) can all produce or exacerbate anxiety symptoms.

### Neurological Conditions
Temporal lobe epilepsy can produce episodes of intense fear, depersonalization, and autonomic symptoms that mimic panic attacks. Vestibular disorders can produce dizziness and associated anxiety.

## Prevalence

### Generalized Anxiety Disorder
The 12-month prevalence of GAD in the United States is approximately 2.9% in adults. Lifetime prevalence is approximately 5.7%. The median age of onset is 30 years, though onset can occur at any age. GAD is approximately twice as common in women as in men.

### Panic Disorder
The 12-month prevalence of panic disorder is approximately 2-3% in adults across the United States and Europe. Lifetime prevalence is approximately 4.7%. The median age of onset is 20-24 years. Like GAD, panic disorder is approximately twice as common in women as in men.

### Anxiety Disorders Overall
Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent class of mental disorders. The 12-month prevalence of any anxiety disorder is approximately 18% in the United States. Anxiety disorders often begin in childhood or adolescence and tend to follow a chronic, waxing-and-waning course if untreated.

## Risk Factors

- **Genetic**: Heritability of anxiety disorders is estimated at 30-40%. Family history of anxiety disorders is a significant risk factor.
- **Temperamental**: Behavioral inhibition in childhood (shyness, withdrawal from novel situations) predicts later anxiety disorders.
- **Environmental**: Adverse childhood experiences, overprotective or critical parenting styles, chronic stress, and traumatic life events.
- **Cognitive**: Tendency toward threat overestimation, intolerance of uncertainty, and attentional bias toward threatening information.
- **Medical**: Chronic medical conditions, particularly those involving pain, respiratory compromise, or cardiac symptoms, increase anxiety disorder risk.
