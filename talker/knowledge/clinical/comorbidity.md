# Psychiatric Comorbidity in Common Mental Health Conditions

## Overview

Comorbidity — the co-occurrence of two or more psychiatric conditions in the same individual — is the rule rather than the exception in mental health. Population studies consistently show that individuals with one mental health condition have significantly elevated odds of meeting criteria for at least one additional condition. Understanding common comorbidity patterns is essential for accurate assessment, because focusing on only one presenting condition can lead to incomplete treatment and poor outcomes.

## Depression and Anxiety

### Prevalence of Co-occurrence

Depression and anxiety disorders are the most frequently co-occurring psychiatric conditions. Approximately 50-60% of individuals with major depressive disorder also meet criteria for at least one anxiety disorder, and approximately 40-50% of individuals with an anxiety disorder have comorbid depression. The lifetime co-occurrence rate is even higher: up to 70% of individuals with MDD will experience a clinically significant anxiety disorder at some point.

### How They Interact

Depression and anxiety share overlapping neurobiology, including dysregulation of serotonergic, noradrenergic, and GABAergic systems, as well as hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis hyperactivity. At the symptom level, they share features such as insomnia, difficulty concentrating, fatigue, and irritability.

However, the conditions also have distinguishing features. Depression is characterized by low positive affect (loss of interest, anhedonia, psychomotor retardation), while anxiety is characterized by high negative affect and physiological hyperarousal. In the tripartite model of emotion, low positive affect differentiates depression from anxiety, high autonomic arousal differentiates anxiety from depression, and high negative affect is common to both.

### Clinical Implications

- **Severity**: Comorbid depression and anxiety is associated with greater symptom severity, longer episode duration, greater functional impairment, and higher suicide risk than either condition alone.
- **Treatment response**: Comorbid cases tend to have slower and less complete treatment response. Both conditions should be addressed in the treatment plan.
- **Sequencing**: Anxiety disorders frequently precede the onset of depression, often by years. Chronic, untreated anxiety is a significant risk factor for subsequent depressive episodes. This has implications for prevention.
- **Assessment**: When a patient presents with depression, anxiety symptoms should be systematically assessed, and vice versa. Screening instruments that cover both domains (such as the PHQ-4, which combines the PHQ-2 and GAD-2) are useful for initial detection.

### Mixed Anxiety and Depressive Disorder

Some individuals present with clinically significant symptoms of both anxiety and depression but do not meet the full diagnostic criteria for either an anxiety disorder or MDD. The ICD-11 recognizes Mixed Anxiety and Depressive Disorder as a diagnosis; the DSM-5 does not include it as a formal diagnosis but acknowledges the concept. These subthreshold presentations are common in primary care and are associated with significant distress and functional impairment.

## PTSD and Depression

### Prevalence of Co-occurrence

Major depressive disorder is the most common comorbidity in PTSD. Approximately 50% of individuals with PTSD also meet criteria for MDD, and in some populations (combat veterans, sexual assault survivors) the rate exceeds 60%. The co-occurrence substantially exceeds what would be expected by chance.

### How They Interact

PTSD and MDD share several overlapping symptoms, particularly within the Criterion D cluster of PTSD (negative alterations in cognitions and mood), which includes diminished interest, emotional numbing, feelings of detachment, and persistent negative emotional states. This overlap can make differential diagnosis challenging.

However, several features distinguish the two conditions:

- **Intrusion symptoms** (flashbacks, trauma-related nightmares, distressing memories of the event) are specific to PTSD and absent in uncomplicated MDD.
- **Avoidance of trauma-related stimuli** is specific to PTSD.
- **Hyperarousal symptoms** (hypervigilance, exaggerated startle) are more characteristic of PTSD, though some arousal symptoms (insomnia, concentration difficulty) overlap.
- **The nature of negative cognitions** differs: in PTSD, negative beliefs tend to center on danger, trust, and self-blame related to the trauma; in MDD, they tend to be broader themes of worthlessness, hopelessness, and global self-criticism.

### Clinical Implications

- **Suicide risk**: Comorbid PTSD and MDD carries a significantly higher suicide risk than either condition alone. Active suicidal ideation should be assessed carefully in all comorbid presentations.
- **Functional impairment**: The combination produces greater disability, higher rates of substance use, and more chronic courses than either condition independently.
- **Treatment**: Evidence-based PTSD treatments (such as Prolonged Exposure and Cognitive Processing Therapy) often produce significant improvement in comorbid depressive symptoms as well. Treating the PTSD may partially resolve the depression, but when depression is severe, concurrent antidepressant treatment may be necessary.

## PTSD and Anxiety Disorders

PTSD frequently co-occurs with other anxiety disorders. Approximately 30-40% of individuals with PTSD also have GAD, and panic disorder co-occurs with PTSD in approximately 10-30% of cases. Social anxiety disorder also co-occurs at elevated rates.

The avoidance and hyperarousal symptoms of PTSD can generate secondary anxiety conditions. For example, an individual who experiences a panic attack during a flashback may develop conditioned panic attacks in similar situations, eventually meeting criteria for panic disorder. Similarly, the hypervigilance of PTSD can generalize into the pervasive worry characteristic of GAD.

## ADHD and Anxiety

### Prevalence of Co-occurrence

Approximately 25-50% of adults with ADHD meet criteria for a comorbid anxiety disorder, most commonly GAD. Conversely, ADHD is found at elevated rates (approximately 10-15%) among individuals presenting to anxiety clinics.

### How They Interact

The relationship between ADHD and anxiety is bidirectional and complex:

- **ADHD-generated anxiety**: Chronic difficulties with organization, time management, and follow-through can produce a secondary anxiety response. The individual is anxious because their ADHD symptoms create constant exposure to stressful situations (missed deadlines, interpersonal conflict, financial disorganization). This anxiety may resemble GAD but is driven by realistic, ADHD-related concerns.
- **Primary comorbid anxiety**: ADHD and anxiety disorders share some genetic liability and neurobiological overlap. In these cases, anxiety exists as an independent condition alongside ADHD.
- **Symptom interaction**: Anxiety can either mask or exacerbate ADHD symptoms. Some anxious individuals with ADHD appear more focused than expected because anxiety-driven hypervigilance partially compensates for inattention. Others find that anxiety worsens their concentration and working memory, amplifying ADHD-related functional impairment.

### Assessment Challenges

Distinguishing ADHD from anxiety (and identifying when both are present) requires careful history-taking:

- **Concentration difficulty** is present in both ADHD and GAD, but in ADHD it is trait-like and lifelong, while in GAD it waxes and wanes with the worry cycle.
- **Restlessness** occurs in both conditions but has different quality: in ADHD it is a constant need for stimulation, while in GAD it is tension-driven.
- **Sleep onset difficulty** in ADHD is typically related to a racing, active mind that is not necessarily worried; in anxiety, difficulty sleeping is driven by worry content.

## ADHD and Depression

Approximately 20-30% of adults with ADHD have comorbid major depression. The relationship is partly mediated by the cumulative effects of ADHD-related impairment: years of underachievement, relationship difficulties, and self-esteem damage can produce a depressive syndrome. However, there is also independent comorbidity — shared genetic risk factors contribute to co-occurrence beyond what environmental mediators alone would predict.

Differentiating ADHD from depression includes attending to the timeline: ADHD symptoms are present from childhood, while depressive episodes have a defined onset. Motivational deficits in depression tend to be global and episodic, while in ADHD, motivation is context-dependent (high for novel or stimulating tasks, low for routine or effortful ones).

## ADHD and Substance Use Disorders

Adults with ADHD have approximately twice the risk of developing a substance use disorder compared to the general population. Impulsivity is a key mediating factor. Common patterns include using stimulants or caffeine for self-medication of inattention, using cannabis or alcohol to manage restlessness and racing thoughts, and impulsive use patterns that escalate. Untreated ADHD in adolescence is a risk factor for earlier onset substance use.

## Implications for Assessment

### Systematic Screening

Because comorbidity is so common, a thorough initial assessment should screen broadly rather than anchoring on the first identified condition. When a patient presents with symptoms of one condition, the assessor should probe for:

- Depression symptoms in anyone presenting with anxiety or PTSD
- Anxiety symptoms in anyone presenting with depression or ADHD
- PTSD in anyone reporting trauma history, regardless of presenting complaint
- ADHD in anyone with lifelong concentration difficulties, even if depression or anxiety seems to explain current symptoms
- Substance use across all presentations

### Diagnostic Hierarchy

When multiple conditions are present, establishing the temporal sequence is important:

- Which symptoms came first? (Lifelong inattention suggests ADHD as primary)
- Did one condition emerge in the context of another? (Depression following years of untreated ADHD)
- Are symptoms better explained by one condition? (Concentration difficulty in depression does not require an additional ADHD diagnosis unless it predates the depression)

### Treatment Planning

The presence of comorbidity affects treatment planning in several ways:

- **Prioritization**: Conditions posing immediate safety risk (suicidal ideation, severe substance dependence) take priority. After stabilization, addressing the condition that most drives functional impairment is generally recommended.
- **Shared mechanisms**: Some treatments address multiple conditions simultaneously (e.g., SSRIs treat both depression and anxiety; trauma-focused therapy often improves comorbid depression).
- **Contraindications**: Some treatments for one condition may worsen another (e.g., stimulants for ADHD may exacerbate anxiety in some individuals; benzodiazepines for anxiety carry risk in individuals with comorbid substance use disorders).
- **Realistic expectations**: Comorbid presentations typically require longer treatment courses, may respond less completely to any single intervention, and benefit from integrated treatment approaches that address multiple conditions.
