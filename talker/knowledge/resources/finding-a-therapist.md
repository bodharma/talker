# Finding a Therapist

## Types of Mental Health Professionals

### Psychiatrists (MD/DO)

Medical doctors who specialize in diagnosing and treating mental health conditions. They can prescribe medication and manage complex medication regimens. Some provide psychotherapy, but many focus on medication management. Typically the most expensive option without insurance.

### Psychologists (PhD/PsyD)

Doctoral-level professionals trained in psychological assessment, testing, and psychotherapy. A PhD typically emphasizes research training, while a PsyD emphasizes clinical practice. Psychologists are well-suited for diagnostic evaluations, neuropsychological testing, and evidence-based therapy. Most cannot prescribe medication (exceptions exist in a few U.S. states and some countries).

### Licensed Clinical Social Workers (LCSW)

Master's-level professionals trained in therapy and often attuned to the social determinants of mental health. LCSWs frequently work in community mental health, hospitals, and private practice. They provide individual, couples, and family therapy.

### Licensed Professional Counselors (LPC/LMHC)

Master's-level professionals trained in counseling and psychotherapy. Titles vary by state (LPC, LMHC, LCPC, LPCC). They provide individual, group, couples, and family therapy for a wide range of mental health concerns.

### Licensed Marriage and Family Therapists (LMFT)

Master's-level professionals with specialized training in relational and family dynamics. While they treat individuals, they are particularly well-suited when relationship issues are a central concern.

### Psychiatric Nurse Practitioners (PMHNP)

Advanced practice nurses with specialized training in psychiatric care. They can prescribe medication and provide therapy. PMHNPs are increasingly common and often have shorter wait times than psychiatrists.

## How to Search for a Therapist

### Online Directories

- **Psychology Today** (psychologytoday.com/us/therapists): The most widely used therapist directory. Filter by insurance, specialty, treatment approach, and demographics.
- **SAMHSA Treatment Locator** (findtreatment.gov): Free tool from the U.S. government for finding treatment facilities and providers.
- **Open Path Collective** (openpathcollective.org): Affordable therapy with sessions ranging from $30-$80.
- **Inclusive Therapists** (inclusivetherapists.com): Directory centered on marginalized communities.

### Through Insurance

Contact your insurance company for a list of in-network providers. Use the member portal or call the number on your insurance card. Ask specifically for providers who are currently accepting new patients, as directories are often outdated. If no in-network providers are available with reasonable wait times, ask about out-of-network benefits or single-case agreements.

### Through Your Primary Care Provider

Your doctor can make referrals and may know which local providers have availability and strong clinical reputations.

### Through Your Employer

Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs) typically offer 3-8 free confidential therapy sessions and can provide referrals for ongoing care.

## What to Look For

### Evidence-Based Training

Ask whether the therapist is trained in evidence-based treatments relevant to your concerns. For depression, look for CBT or behavioral activation. For anxiety, look for CBT with exposure components. For PTSD, look for CPT, PE, or EMDR. For ADHD, look for CBT adapted for adult ADHD. A therapist who can name their approach and explain why it fits your situation is a good sign.

### Experience with Your Concerns

A therapist who regularly treats people with your specific condition will be more effective than a generalist. Ask how much of their caseload involves your type of concern.

### Good Fit

The therapeutic relationship is one of the strongest predictors of treatment outcome. After an initial session, consider: Did you feel heard and understood? Did the therapist explain their approach clearly? Were they warm but also direct and structured? Did they set clear expectations for treatment? It is entirely appropriate to try a few therapists before committing.

## Questions to Ask a Potential Therapist

- What is your experience treating my specific concern?
- What treatment approach do you use, and what does the evidence say about it?
- How do you typically structure treatment? How many sessions should I expect?
- How do you measure progress?
- What are your fees, and do you accept my insurance?
- What is your cancellation policy?
- Are you available for between-session contact in case of a crisis?
- Do you assign homework or exercises between sessions?

## Insurance and Cost Considerations

### In-Network Providers

These have contracted rates with your insurance, resulting in lower out-of-pocket costs. You typically pay a copay ($20-$50) or coinsurance (10-30% of the session fee) after meeting your deductible.

### Out-of-Network Providers

You pay the full session fee upfront and may submit claims for partial reimbursement if your plan includes out-of-network mental health benefits. A superbill (itemized receipt) from your therapist is used for this process. Reimbursement rates vary widely.

### Sliding Scale

Many therapists offer reduced fees based on income. Ask directly whether a sliding scale is available. Community mental health centers and training clinics at universities often offer therapy at significantly reduced rates.

### Parity Laws

The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act requires most insurance plans to cover mental health services at the same level as medical services. If your insurance imposes more restrictive limitations on mental health coverage, you may be able to appeal.

## Online Therapy Options

Telehealth therapy has become widely available and research shows it is comparably effective to in-person therapy for most conditions. Benefits include greater access in rural or underserved areas, reduced barriers related to transportation and scheduling, and access to specialists who may not practice locally.

Dedicated platforms such as those offered by major telehealth companies provide convenient access, though verifying therapist credentials and evidence-based training remains important. Many traditional in-person therapists also now offer video sessions.

Online therapy may not be ideal for severe symptoms requiring intensive monitoring, active suicidal ideation, or situations where privacy at home is not possible.

## If You Cannot Find a Therapist

Therapist shortages are real, and wait times can be long. If immediate access is difficult:

- Ask to be placed on cancellation lists for quicker openings
- Consider group therapy, which is effective for many conditions and often has shorter wait times
- Use evidence-based self-help resources (books, structured online programs) as a bridge
- Contact your insurance company about out-of-network exceptions if in-network options are unavailable
- Explore training clinics at local universities where supervised graduate students provide therapy at reduced cost
- Contact SAMHSA's helpline (1-800-662-4357) for referral assistance
